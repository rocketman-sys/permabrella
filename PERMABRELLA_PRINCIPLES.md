# PERMABRELLA_PRINCIPLES

## Purpose

PermaBrella exists to increase the real-world capacity of the Northern Rivers to grow food, share resources, strengthen connection, and build resilience through practical local action.

It is not here to maximise content, traffic, or passive engagement.

It is here to help activate useful relationships and real-world outcomes.

---

## Core Design Test

Every feature, page, category, workflow, or partnership should be tested against this question:

**Does this help increase local food-growing capacity, participation, reciprocity, or resilience in a meaningful way?**

If not, it is probably noise, overhead, or drift.

---

## Product Principles

### 1. Real yield over digital theatre
PermaBrella should be judged by what it enables in the world, not by how busy or polished it looks.

Useful signs of yield include:
- more food being grown
- more people participating
- more tools, land, skills, and surplus being shared
- more local events and collaborations
- stronger local trust and resilience

---

### 2. Connection over accumulation
The goal is not to hoard information.

The goal is to help useful things find each other:
- people to projects
- growers to land
- surplus to need
- skills to opportunities
- events to participants
- knowledge to action

A static directory is not enough. The value is in movement and activation.

---

### 3. Hyper-local first
PermaBrella should work at the scale of a real region, town, watershed, or neighbourhood.

It should feel grounded in the Northern Rivers:
- climate
- soils
- flood and fire realities
- local culture
- existing groups and initiatives
- practical local conditions

Abstraction is not the point. Regional usefulness is.

---

### 4. Participation over spectatorship
The platform should gently move people from passive interest toward active involvement.

Good features help people:
- attend
- host
- ask
- offer
- exchange
- learn
- volunteer
- grow

Bad features encourage endless browsing without action.

---

### 5. Clarity builds trust
Warmth matters, but clarity matters more.

People should be able to quickly understand:
- what something is
- who it is for
- whether it is human or AI-authored
- what action is possible next
- what expectations apply

Confusion creates drift. Clarity creates trust.

---

### 6. Human observation stays primary
AI can assist with:
- synthesis
- organisation
- pattern recognition
- drafting
- summarising
- discoverability

But AI should not masquerade as lived local knowledge.

Grounded human observation remains primary, especially for:
- land
- weather
- water
- plants
- local context
- practical judgment

AI should be visibly distinct and used in support of human sense-making, not in place of it.

---

### 7. Distinguish authorship clearly
Human and AI-authored content should be visibly marked.

This is not a cosmetic issue. It is part of the trust architecture.

At minimum:
- Human content should be labelled as human
- AI-assisted or AI-authored content should be labelled clearly
- users should not have to guess

---

### 8. Reciprocity matters
A living community is not built only on asking.

PermaBrella should support a culture where offering, contributing, and helping are normal.

This does not mean forcing balance in every moment.
It means designing for mutuality over time.

---

### 9. Reduce fragmentation
Many communities already contain the ingredients for resilience, but they are disconnected.

PermaBrella should reduce fragmentation between:
- people and land
- knowledge and practice
- groups and each other
- offerings and those who need them
- intention and implementation

If a feature increases fragmentation, complexity, or siloing, it should be questioned.

---

### 10. Support modular decentralisation
PermaBrella should not try to own everything.

It should function as a connective layer that supports many local nodes:
- community gardens
- seed savers
- permablitz groups
- land-sharing arrangements
- neighbourhood networks
- workshops
- food resilience initiatives

The platform should strengthen distributed capacity rather than centralise control.

---

### 11. Useful friction, minimal bureaucracy
There should be enough structure to support:
- trust
- safety
- relevance
- accountability

But not so much process that participation dies.

The ideal is useful friction, not administrative drag.

---

### 12. The map must serve the territory
PermaBrella is not the thing itself.

It is a layer that should help people engage more effectively with the real world:
- gardens
- soil
- water
- seeds
- relationships
- events
- shared work
- local resilience

If the digital layer starts becoming more important than the lived layer, something has gone wrong.

---

## Practical Feature Priorities

Prioritise features that:
- help people find local events and opportunities
- support exchange of skills, seeds, tools, land, and surplus
- make practical knowledge accessible
- lower the barrier to participation
- strengthen trust and transparency
- support local initiative without over-centralising control

Be cautious with features that:
- reward noise over usefulness
- create passive-scroll behaviour
- add maintenance burden without clear yield
- centralise authority unnecessarily
- blur AI and human contributions
- look clever but do not improve real-world capacity

---

## A Simple Internal Check

Before building or approving something, ask:

1. What real-world yield does this create?
2. Who does it help participate?
3. What fragmentation does it reduce?
4. Does it increase trust or confusion?
5. Does it move people toward action or just more content?

If the answers are weak, the idea probably needs to be simplified or dropped.
